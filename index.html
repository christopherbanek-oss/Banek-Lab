01 The Lab

Sensory nerves of the kidney are unusually persuasive organs — they listen, they signal, and when miscalibrated, they reshape the body's most consequential systems.

The Banek Lab investigates how peripheral nerves control cardiovascular and renal function in health and disease.

  • UMN Medical School
  • Department of Surgery
  • Division of Autonomic Neuromodulation

Our current projects focus on the role of peripheral sensory nerves in the development and maintenance of two conditions: hypertension and polycystic kidney disease. Sensory nerves in the kidney are known to drive the progression of several experimental models of cardiovascular and renal disease, largely through a pathological increase in renal afferent nerve activity.

The mechanisms triggering this elevated activity — and its downstream physiological consequences — remain poorly understood. To address these questions, we combine chronic telemetry-based recordings with acute electrophysiological preparations, allowing us to track changes in nerve activity alongside the corresponding cardiovascular and renal phenotypes. We complement these approaches with molecular studies aimed at identifying the specific proteins and non-peptide signals that drive aberrant sensory nerve activation.

03 Research

We work along three coordinated arms — each pulling at a different thread of the same nervous-system puzzle.

i.

Renal sensory nerves and the progression of hypertension.

We employ a series of surgical studies to isolate renal sensory nerves before or after hypertension is established, performing targeted denervations of the kidney. In addition to monitoring cardiovascular and renal responses to denervation, we record the pathological renal sensory nerve signal directly to investigate how it codes for elevated blood pressure.

Hypertension Renal Denervation In Vivo Telemetry
ii.

Renal denervation as a treatment for polycystic kidney disease.

Through targeted renal nerve ablations, we evaluate the specific role of renal nerves in the progression of polycystic kidney disease. We are pursuing this treatment in both forms of the disease — autosomal dominant (ADPKD) and autosomal recessive (ARPKD) — to understand which patients might benefit and how the intervention reshapes disease trajectory.

ADPKD ARPKD Translational
iii.

Peripheral inflammation and aberrant sensory nerve activity.

Another arm of the lab directly records the interaction between inflammatory mediators — cytokines and related signals — and aberrant sensory nerve activity in models of cardiovascular and kidney disease. We measure these interactions through multi-unit nerve recordings in vivo and whole-cell patch-clamp electrophysiology of isolated sensory neurons.
